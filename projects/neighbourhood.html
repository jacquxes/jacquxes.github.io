Neighbourhood: From vague problem to meaningful product

A community networking product designed to help neighbours connect through shared interests.

Role One of three designers, focusing on research synthesis, insight-driven product design, and user-tested enhancements
Methods Design sprint, field research, prototyping, user testing
Overview

The project

This project used the design sprint process to respond to the vague problem of "loneliness in communities", focusing on how it might be addressed through shared interests. Design sprint: 4 days (synthesis, rapid product design, user testing).

Duration

4-day design sprint

Team

Solo designer

Output

High-fidelity prototype

Discovery

The vague problem

We started with an even vaguer brief: "How might we improve neighbourhoods in Singapore?"

When digging around in general discussion forums and Reddit communities with 23K+ members, I found that it isn't just loneliness — it's that people want to connect with neighbours who share their interests. Simply being neighbours wasn't enough of a connection to make it feel safe.

I had to narrow down the app from a general neighbourhood improvement tool to one that solves a very specific problem — and one that was passably explorable.

Secondary research

Forums showed people wanted deeper connection through shared interests — not just surface-level proximity. Communities with 23K+ members still felt disconnected.

User interviews

Interviews with 5 participants confirmed: people are more likely to connect with a neighbour if they already know they share something in common.

"How might we easily find neighbours with similar interests to connect?"

— Design pivot question that reframed the entire project
Research

Key insights

From my research, three key insights emerged that shaped our design strategy.

01

Relationships feel like work

Maintaining relationships in a community is based on an invisible cost-benefit analysis — people need to feel the payoff is worth the social effort.

02

People stick to what's familiar

Existing relationships with family and close friends already fill connection needs. Breaking into a new community requires a compelling enough hook.

03

Shared interests drive connection

Community is something at a human scale — neighbours connected by a hobby or interest are far more likely to build meaningful connections.

🏸
The design pivot

"With this pivot, we narrowed their asking for neighbours to start connecting through shared interests. If you can play badminton at your doorstep, you're more likely to start, not 30 minutes away."

Design

Neighbourhood

A community product that connects people in Singapore with their neighbours through shared interests. The design focused on three core principles that made connection feel effortless and low-stakes.

Familiar faces, shared interests

People connect to people, not places

By suggesting nearby neighbours with compatible interests — a mutual contact, sport or activity in common — users can discover a familiar face. The product leans into recommendations that make the first message feel natural, not awkward.

Small steps to connection

Instead of "friend me", start with small, manageable actions

Rather than forcing connection, the app suggests bite-size actions such as joining a group run, sharing a recipe, or co-hosting a local event. Small actions build trust before bigger connections form.

Connections that keep growing

After meeting, we suggest people expand to their neighbourhood

After meeting, we suggest people add new members to their neighbourhood activity — organically extending their local network and reinforcing the habit of connection, making every interaction a gateway to more.

Outcome

Vague problem to meaningful product

This project started with an extremely open brief and no clear direction. The resulting product doesn't try to force community — it instead creates the conditions for it to happen naturally. By starting with shared interests, reducing friction, and building momentum through small actions, Neighbourhood makes community feel possible again.

The resulting product doesn't try to force community. It instead creates the conditions for it to happen — reducing friction at every step from first discovery to ongoing connection.

Reflection

Got a vague problem to solve?

The most important skill in this project wasn't design — it was reframing. The initial brief was so open it was almost impossible to act on. Narrowing it down to a specific, solvable question unlocked everything else.

Start narrow, not broad

A vague problem is not an invitation to solve everything. It's an invitation to find the one thing worth solving. The more specific the problem, the more meaningful the solution.

Research before designing

The pivot from "neighbourhood improvement" to "interest-based connection" only happened because I spent time in real communities — reading forums, conducting interviews, and listening without an agenda.

Reduce friction at every step

Every additional step between a user wanting to connect and actually connecting is a drop-off point. The product only worked when we made each action feel small and safe.

Design for behaviour change

The goal was never "use the app more" — it was to change a real behaviour: talking to your neighbours. A product that solves a meaningful problem in the real world is the only kind worth building.
