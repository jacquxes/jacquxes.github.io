Grow with CPF: Reframing retirement for the next generation

Helping young people overcome financial paralysis by reframing abstract long-term goals into immediate, actionable progress.

Role One of four designers, focusing on product strategy and data visualisations
Methods Lean UX, user research, prototyping
Overview

The project

CPF is Singapore's mandatory savings scheme, but young adults rarely engage with it beyond the payslip deduction. This project tackled the gap between people knowing CPF exists and actually caring enough to grow it actively. The goal: reframe retirement planning as something relevant, achievable, and even motivating for people in their 20s and 30s.

Duration

8 weeks

Methods

Interviews, surveys, prototyping

Output

Tested hi-fi prototype

Problem

The challenge

Understanding what stands between young Singaporeans and meaningful engagement with their retirement product — and how to move the needle on that.

school

Abstract concept

Retirement feels too far away to be motivating. Young people struggle to connect future needs to present actions when the timeline spans decades.

trending_up

Mixed emotions

Money anxiety, FOMO, and a general distrust of government products made CPF feel like something that happens to you, not something you actively manage.

flag

Awareness gap

Despite being mandatory, many young adults didn't know what their CPF balance actually meant, what the accounts were for, or how to grow them intentionally.

Research

Understanding our user

I conducted in-depth interviews with 8 participants aged 24–35, alongside a survey of 47 respondents. The goal was to understand what retirement actually meant to them — not what the system said it should mean.

Three patterns emerged consistently across all participants — regardless of income, education, or financial literacy:

👁️ Inventory mismatch

People regularly check their bank balance but almost never check their CPF — because it doesn't feel like "real money they can use".

🎯 Goal resonance

When shown concrete life goals (flat, travel, retiring by 55), people immediately became more engaged — goals made the abstraction feel real.

📊 Bonus insights

Learning about CPF's bonus interest rates through peers was a consistent trigger for increased engagement — people responded more to discovery than education.

"I only really started thinking about my CPF after my friend told me about the bonus interest. Nobody tells you this stuff."

— Participant, 29, Software Engineer

Design

Grow with CPF

The product parallels the user's life journey — not CPF's account structure. Instead of showing balances and rules, it shows life goals and the path to reaching them. Three core features address the three biggest barriers to engagement.

Help users take the first step

Help users take things one step at a time, so goals feel reachable

The onboarding flow anchors around a life goal the user already has — a flat, early retirement, a safety net. From there, it back-calculates what they'd need to do each month. One action, not a system to learn.

Assure users that their goals are within reach

Show users what their CPF can actually do for them

An interactive projection tool shows how different top-up amounts change the retirement picture — not in CPF-speak, but in life terms: "At this rate, you could retire at 58." Making the future tangible makes it actionable.

Make learning finances less intimidating

Introducing CPF's hidden features — at exactly the right moment

Instead of a help centre, contextual education surfaces when it's relevant. When a user is looking at their OA balance, they see a tip about the bonus interest rate. When they top up, they learn about tax relief. Discovery, not instruction.

Outcome

Make the most of your data

The project demonstrated that the biggest barrier to CPF engagement wasn't awareness — it was relevance. When the product spoke to people's actual goals instead of the system's structure, behaviour changed. Users went from passive observers to active participants in their own financial futures.

Reflection

Got a data problem to solve?

The data background turned out to be the most valuable thing I brought to this project. Not because I built charts — but because I knew when a number was meaningful and when it was noise. The hardest design decision was removing data, not adding it.

Data without context is decoration

Showing someone their CPF balance changes nothing. Showing them what that balance means for their retirement at 55 changes everything. Always ask: what decision does this data support?

Reframe the problem before designing

The brief was "improve CPF engagement". The real problem was "retirement feels irrelevant to young people". The solution only worked because we solved the right problem.

Emotion drives financial behaviour

Every financial decision has an emotional component. Designing for that — anxiety, aspiration, FOMO — was more effective than designing for information alone.

Teach through doing, not reading

Contextual tips at the moment of action outperformed any onboarding tutorial or help centre. People learn financial concepts when they're already motivated to act.
