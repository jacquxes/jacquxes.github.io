UX Research · 2025

UX Research on DBS PayLah!

A mixed-methods study combining interviews, surveys, and heuristic evaluation to uncover where PayLah!'s payment and transfer experience breaks down — and what to do about it.

Role Solo UX Researcher
Methods Interviews · Survey · Heuristic evaluation
Output Research report + design recommendations
Overview

The project

PayLah! is DBS's digital wallet — used alongside PayNow for transfers and payments in Singapore. Despite its widespread adoption, the app had friction points that weren't well understood. This project set out to identify those areas through a structured, multi-method research process: first understanding how real users experience the app, then evaluating it against usability principles, and finally translating findings into concrete design recommendations that were tested with users.

Approach

Qualitative + quantitative

Research methods

Interviews, survey, heuristic eval, usability testing

Focus areas

Money transfer, QR payment, app access

Problem

Where does PayLah! lose its users?

PayLah! and PayNow serve overlapping but distinct use cases — and users often switch between them without a clear mental model of why. The friction that results tends to surface in the same three moments: sending money, making a QR payment, and simply getting into the app. The goal of this research was to make those friction points visible and quantifiable, and to understand whether they were being felt broadly or only by specific user types.

Research questions

What prevents users from completing transfers and payments smoothly? Where do they feel uncertain, frustrated, or unsupported? Are these issues consistent across different user profiles?

Scope

Three core flows were examined: transferring money to a contact, making a payment via QR code, and accessing the app reliably. Each was evaluated both through user accounts and through heuristic analysis.

Research

A mixed-methods approach

No single method gives the full picture. Interviews reveal the why — the mental models, frustrations, and workarounds users have built. Surveys reveal the how common — whether those experiences are isolated or widespread. Together, they let research findings be both rich and credible.

Qualitative

User interviews

In-depth conversations with PayLah! users to understand how they think about money transfer and payment, what trips them up, and what workarounds they've developed.

5 participants across different usage patterns Semi-structured interview format Thematic analysis to surface patterns Questions covering transfer flow, QR payment, and app access
Quantitative

Survey

A structured survey to test whether patterns found in interviews were broadly shared — validating qualitative findings with measurable frequency data across a wider sample.

Broader sample beyond interview participants Likert-scale and multiple choice questions Focused on frequency, confidence, and task completion Results used to prioritise which issues to address first
Research principle

"Interviews tell you what's wrong. Surveys tell you how wrong it is. You need both to make a case for change."

Findings

Research insights

Three themes emerged consistently across both the interviews and the survey — pointing to the same underlying issues regardless of user profile or payment context.

01

Users want to avoid making costly mistakes

90% of survey respondents cited "keying in the wrong amount" as a top concern, and 80% flagged "transferring to the wrong person". As one participant put it: "Sometimes you type too fast — instead of transferring $3.57 you transfer $357." — Data scientist, 36.

02

Users want to complete payments quickly and successfully

70% flagged "unable to scan QR code" and 50% flagged "insufficient wallet balance" as top concerns. As one participant explained: "If my phone cannot scan the QR code, I'm concerned it won't work — especially when there is a long queue behind me." — Market research executive, 34.

03

New and experienced users have fundamentally different needs

Experienced users had built mental shortcuts that masked friction. New users encountered the same flows as significantly harder. A single design cannot optimally serve both without explicitly accounting for this split.

💡
Key research insight

"The biggest fears aren't about the app being hard to use — they're about the consequences of using it wrong. Design that prevents errors matters more than design that looks clean."

Analysis

Heuristic evaluation

To complement the user research, I evaluated PayLah! against established usability heuristics across the three core flows. This gave a structured, principle-based view of where the interface violated good design practice — independent of what users said, but consistent with it.

Flow 01

Transferring money

Current experience

Transfer entry point not prominent on home screen — requires scanning to locate

Contact selection mixes PayLah! and phone contacts without clear distinction

Amount entry lacks real-time balance visibility — users can't easily check limits

-

Suggested experience

Surface transfer CTA prominently with persistent bottom navigation

Separate PayLah! contacts from phone contacts with clear visual grouping

Show available balance inline during amount entry

Flow 02

Making payment via QR

Current experience

QR scanner buried inside the app — not accessible from the lock screen or home

Post-payment confirmation screen disappears too quickly and lacks a persistent receipt

No way to distinguish between different QR payment types at a glance

-

Suggested experience

Add QR scanner shortcut to lock screen widget and home screen

Make payment confirmation persistent and accessible from transaction history immediately

Label QR payment types clearly (PayNow, NETS, etc.) before confirming

Flow 03

Accessing the app

Current experience

Biometric login inconsistently offered — some users not prompted to set it up

Session timeout too aggressive, frequently interrupting mid-task flows

Error messages on login failure are generic and don't help users self-recover

-

Suggested experience

Proactively prompt biometric setup after first successful login for all users

Extend session timeout for active use; only lapse during genuine inactivity

Replace generic error messages with specific next steps for each failure mode

Design

Recommendations and usability testing

Findings from the research and heuristic evaluation were translated into design changes, which were then tested with participants in four structured tasks. The goal was to validate whether the changes improved task completion and reduced the confusion identified in the research phase.

Task 1 — Transfer flow

Restructured transfer entry and contact selection

The transfer action was elevated to a persistent home screen CTA. Contact selection was reorganised to clearly separate PayLah! contacts from the phone book, with recent transfers surfaced at the top. Usability testing confirmed users completed the task faster and with fewer hesitations.

Task 2 — QR payment

Surfaced QR scanner and improved payment confirmation

The QR scanner was added to the home screen and accessible from the lock screen. After payment, a persistent confirmation screen was introduced that remained accessible from the transaction history. Participants reported significantly higher confidence that payments had gone through successfully.

Task 3 — New user onboarding

Differentiated experience for new vs. familiar users

A lightweight onboarding layer was introduced for first-time and infrequent users, surfacing key shortcuts and labelling actions that experienced users had learned to navigate. Crucially, this layer disappears after the first few sessions — so it doesn't add friction for users who already know the app.

Task 4 — App access

Improved biometric setup and session handling

Biometric setup was made a proactive prompt rather than a buried setting. Session timeout logic was adjusted to only trigger on genuine inactivity — not mid-flow. Error messages were rewritten to give users specific recovery steps. Testing showed a marked reduction in login-related drop-offs.

Reflection

What this research revealed

This project reinforced why mixed-methods research matters. The interviews alone might have generated a long list of frustrations without a sense of priority. The survey alone might have confirmed issues existed but not explained why. Together, they produced findings that were both specific enough to design from and credible enough to advocate for.

Qual tells the story. Quant makes the case.

Interview findings are compelling to designers. Survey data is compelling to stakeholders. Running both gives you the language to influence whoever you're presenting to — and the credibility to back it up.

Heuristic evaluation is a research method, not a shortcut

Done rigorously — evaluated against specific principles, with both current and suggested states — a heuristic evaluation produces actionable findings without requiring a full round of user testing at that stage.

Design for two users, not one

The new user vs. experienced user split is one of the most commonly overlooked distinctions in app design. The same screen that feels intuitive to someone who's used it for a year can be opaque to someone opening it for the first time.

Test your recommendations, not just your findings

Research that ends at insights is only half done. Usability testing the recommended designs closed the loop — confirming which changes actually improved the experience and which needed further iteration.
